# Grainlab Century 100 film canon

`century-100-v1` is a researched, versioned canon of 100 photographic and motion-picture materials introduced across roughly the last century. It is not presented as a worldwide unit-sales chart: no defensible public dataset compares every still, cinema, professional, consumer, state-manufactured, private-label, and discontinued stock across 100 years and every market.

The ranking is therefore a transparent curatorial instrument. It weighs historical and cultural use, technical influence, geographic/manufacturer breadth, process diversity, influence on later visual practice, continued relevance, and the quality of surviving manufacturer or archival documentation. A high rank means “important to understand when building a broad film language,” not “objectively better.”

## Coverage

- 100 ranked materials: 45 monochrome and 55 color.
- Process families: 43 silver B&W, 25 C-41/chromogenic, 16 reversal, 14 ECN-2-style cinema negative, and two instant/print materials.
- Manufacturing traditions represented: United States, Japan, United Kingdom, Germany/East Germany, Belgium, Czech Republic, Italy, Austria, Ukraine/former USSR, and China.
- Capture contexts: consumer snapshots, portrait and wedding work, press/reportage, fine-art and technical recording, aerial infrared, studio and landscape transparency, narrative cinema, small-gauge cinema, instant proofing, and peel-apart art practice.

Kodak/Eastman and Fujifilm occupy many places because their materials dominated large parts of global still and motion-picture production and because their technical lineages contain genuinely different process generations. The final 42 places broaden the canon through Ilford/Harman, Agfa/Ansco, ORWO, Foma, ADOX, Rollei, CineStill, Ferrania, Lomography, Svema, Shanghai, Lucky, Japan Camera Hunter, and Polaroid.

## How each emulation is constructed

Every stock file is an executable research card, not only a name and five sliders. It defines:

1. **Digital starting posture.** Prefer a neutral RAW development. For iPhone HEIC/JPEG or camera JPEG, minimize computational HDR, local tone mapping, vivid styles, skin smoothing, denoise, clarity, and sharpening. Map wide-gamut/HDR input into a stable SDR image without clipping before asking the film model to shape it.
2. **Scene/capture stage.** Decode sRGB into linear light, place exposure, and apply stock-specific channel sensitivity or monochrome spectral weighting. This is where daylight/tungsten balance and ortho/extended-red response begin.
3. **Emulsion stage.** Apply toe, development gamma, shoulder, density-dependent color crossover, saturation compression, and the selected silver/dye image structure.
4. **Lab and optics stage.** Apply process-appropriate fog, local density contrast, retained silver where relevant, and thresholded highlight scatter. Remjet-backed ECN-2 stocks stay restrained; remjet-removed still-film interpretations can halate more strongly.
5. **Scan/output stage.** Keep cinema negatives comparatively open for grading, preserve reversal-film black density and limited latitude, and treat instant materials as positive systems rather than blurred C-41.
6. **Image structure.** Use deterministic, non-periodic, signal-dependent correlated grain over the whole image. Metallic silver is crisper and more neutral; dye-cloud texture is softer with mostly shared density variation and weak chroma-layer independence.

The numerical profiles are bounded artistic approximations informed by published characteristics and process knowledge. They are not digitized manufacturer sensitometric curves, spectral sensitivity measurements, scanner profiles, or endorsements. A stock’s appearance still changes with batch, format, lens, filtration, exposure, developer/process control, printing path, scanner, operator, storage, and aging.

Visible-RGB input creates a hard limit: infrared and extended-red profiles cannot reconstruct wavelengths removed by a DSLR/iPhone IR-cut filter, and no profile can recover a channel clipped or discarded by computational processing. Those profiles are tonal analogies, clearly labeled in their dossiers.

## Source policy

Technical claims should prefer manufacturer product sheets, processing manuals, sensitometric workbooks, research papers, and company histories. When a manufacturer has disappeared or removed its literature, a scan of the original manufacturer publication or a recognized institutional/material archive is labeled as such. Product marketing, community examples, and recollection can guide aesthetic interpretation, but they do not silently become measured facts.

Every profile stores its own HTTPS sources and verification date. Current anchor collections include Kodak’s professional-film resources and motion chronology/processing manuals, Fujifilm’s professional data guide and product bulletins, Harman/Ilford technical downloads, Foma/ADOX/Rollei/ORWO manufacturer data, CineStill and Lomography process/product literature, Shanghai’s manufacturer history, and the Harvard Polaroid Corporation archive. This document describes the shared method; the dossier beside each stock is the claim-level handoff.

## Maintaining the canon

Each stock is an individual JSON file under `ui/film-stocks/`. Canon entries additionally define:

```json
{
  "catalog": {
    "edition": "century-100-v1",
    "rank": 14,
    "introduced": "1998",
    "origin": "United States",
    "rationale": "Why this stock belongs in the canon."
  }
}
```

The Tauri build validates that the edition contains exactly 100 unique ranks from 1 through 100. New experimental or house stocks can still be added without a `catalog` block. A future curatorial revision should use a new edition identifier and a written migration rationale rather than silently reshuffling this one.
